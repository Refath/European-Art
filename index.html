Liberty Leading the People [ROMANTIC]

Eugene Delacroix's Liberty Leading the People celebrates the overthrow of King Charles X of France during the 1830 revolutions. The woman seizing the French flag represents liberty -- an often abstract ideal personified by the french woman -- guiding the masses. The mounds of fallen comrades demonstrates revolution cannot survive isolated; it is built and supported by the people, akin to Newton's "I stand upon the shoulders of giants".

Mona Lisa [RENAISSANCE]

Perhaps the most celebrated painting of all time, Mona Lisa is a product of the renaissance. Painted early 16th century, it emphasizes the individual through Mona Lisa's fascinating eyes, and her collected posture. Mona Lisa seemingly breaks the fourth wall, aware that she is being painted, and staring directly at the viewer. The painting was popularized further after it's famous disseapearance from the Louvre in 1911.

Triumph of the Immaculate [BAROQUE]

Revolutionary for it's overwhelming barrage of characters, Paolo de Matteis' Triumph of the Immaculate is a service to the Jesuits' attempt to re-catholocize Europe during the counter-reformations.

Wanderer above the Sea of Fog [ROMANTIC]

A true ambassador of Romantic art, Wanderer above the Sea of Fog not only depicts the beautiful harmony between man and nature, but also the self-intreospective nature of the wanderer (or hiker, in German). The breathtaking scenery arrests the viewer's attention; the fateful convergence between land and sky depicted in striking contrast: the dark black of the mountains eventually merge with the light blue of the sky.

David [RENAISSANCE]

Michaelangelo's David is characteristic of Renaissance: a valiant, collected warrior prepared to fight a force larger than man: Goliath. David stands nude, depicted in contrappasto, with his bow drawn and ready to attack.

Boarding the Tripolitan Gunboat [ROMANTIC]

A classic romantic peice, Boarding the Tripolitan Gunboat illustrates the toil of revolution, and it's lofty ideals of liberty as were present during the revolutions of 1848.

Oath of the Horatii [NEOCLASSICAL]

A masterpeice of Neoclassical art, Jacque Louis David's Oath of the Horatii depicts the patriotism and sacrifice through the movement of the father holding out the swords from his sons. The painting depicts a Roman legend about a fight between two cities. The Roman salute was popularized through this painting through the three sons' salute of their father.

Arnolfini Portrait [RENAISSANCE]

An absolute classic of the Renaissance, The Arnolfini Portrait uniquely depicts the contract of marriage. The painting was revolutionary for its time, creatively using a mirror to extend depth and lighting to produce the interior comfort of a home.

Interior of a Tailor Shop [REALISM]

Interior of a Tailor Shop is Exemplary of the precision and accuracy of the paintings of the industrial revolution. The tailors are not glorified, but depicted frankly through the toil of their hard work.

The Birth of Venus [RENAISSANCE]

A revolutionary masterpeice which depicted the arrival of the roman goddess Venus upon earth. Birth of Venus set the precedent for many other renaissance painting through Venus' contrappasto pose, and the nudity of all but one figure in the painting.

Barricade of Rue Sufflot [ROMANTIC]

Horace Vennet's Barricade of Rue Sufflot is a classic romantic peice that conveys the toil and pain behind the lofty goal of liberty. It illustrates the Battle at Soufflot barricades in Paris during the Revolutions of 1848.

WHO, WHAT, WHERE, WHEN, AND WHY?

Who's the easiest: Me, Refath Bari. What? This website serves as a tribute to the transformation of European art throughout the course of four centuries of revolution, famine, disease, war, and tranquility. Where? The train and my room. When? The night before. I'm a notorious procrastinator. Why? To get extra credit. On a more serious note, I hope this websites enables future students to understand that art is a visual accomplice to history; It is a reaction of an event by those who experienced it, and those who look on from the future.
